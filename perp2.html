Ted Bundy

American Serial Killer | Active 1974-1978 | Executed 1989

Early Life & Family Background

Birth Name: Theodore Robert Cowell (later changed to Bundy)

Born: November 24, 1946 in Burlington, Vermont

Childhood Trauma: Bundy was raised believing his grandparents were his parents and his mother was his sister. This early deception may have contributed to his psychological development.

Education: Earned a psychology degree from the University of Washington in 1972. Later attended law school at the University of Utah.

Early Signs: As a teenager, Bundy exhibited disturbing behaviors including voyeurism and theft, though he maintained a charming public persona.

Psychological Profile

Diagnosed Conditions:

  • Antisocial Personality Disorder
  • Narcissistic Personality Disorder
  • Possible Psychopathy (PCL-R score 39/40)

Key Traits:

  • Extreme charm and intelligence (IQ estimated at 136)
  • Complete lack of remorse or empathy
  • Compulsive need for control and domination
  • Sexual sadism (derived pleasure from victims' suffering)

Manipulative Behavior: Bundy famously acted as his own attorney during trial, using the opportunity to manipulate media attention and flirt with female spectators.

Murder Methods & Signature Behaviors

Bundy's killing methods evolved but maintained distinctive patterns:

Victim Selection

Primarily targeted young, attractive white women with long dark hair parted in the middle

Modus Operandi

Used charm and fake injuries to gain victims' trust before attacking

Signature

Returned to victims' corpses for sexual acts and kept trophies

Escalation: Began with blitz attacks, progressed to elaborate ruses, and eventually committed home invasions.

Capture & Execution

Arrest: First arrested in 1975 for attempted kidnapping in Utah. Later escaped twice before final capture in Florida in 1978.

Confessions: In final days, confessed to 30 murders across 7 states, though actual count may be higher.

Execution: Electrocuted at Florida State Prison on January 24, 1989 after numerous appeals.

Legacy: Bundy's case revolutionized criminal profiling and helped establish the FBI's Behavioral Analysis Unit.
